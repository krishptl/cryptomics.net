The problem

Genomic data powers precision medicine, drug discovery, risk prediction, consumer genomics, and forensics. At the same time, genomes are uniquely identifying and effectively immutable once exposed, harm cannot be undone and re-identification risk only grows.

Locked away or overexposed

Today, institutions face a false choice. Genomic data is either locked down, slowing research and clinical progress, or broadly shared, creating high risk of re-identification, discrimination, and misuse.

Monolithic files, blunt access

Existing security models treat genomes as monolithic files (VCF, BAM, FASTA) with all-or-nothing access. They ignore region-level risk (e.g., BRCA1 vs. neutral regions) and lack role-, purpose-, and time-bounded controls.

Policy on paper, not in code

HIPAA, GDPR, IRB, and consent forms live on paper or in PDFs. Enforcement is manual and after the fact, rather than encoded directly into how keys are issued and data is accessed.

Security that users work around

Crypto, key management, and file formats are invisible, brittle, or bolted on. Analysts, clinicians, and investigators work around security controls to get their job done, eroding trust for participants and regulators.

Our approach

Cryptomics combines modern cryptography, fine-grained data architecture, and machine-enforceable policy to make region-level access control feel like a normal analysis workflow.

Region-aware cryptography

  • Per-gene / per-interval keys (e.g., BRCA1, APOE, panels) instead of whole-file secrets.
  • Purpose-limited, time-bounded key material using modern primitives (e.g., AES-GCM, KMS).
  • Protocols where computations run on encrypted genomes; raw data is rarely decrypted.

Fine-grained data architecture

  • Refactor genomes from monolithic VCF/BAM into addressable units: genes, regions, panels.
  • Region-level access policies and audit logs: who touched which locus, when, and why.
  • Designed for clinical pipelines, research workflows, and forensic queries.

Policy-aligned by default

  • Translate consent, IRB, and regulatory requirements into machine-enforceable policies.
  • Keys are gated by policy decisions, not just network perimeter or file permissions.
  • Audit trails that stand up to regulators, ethics boards, and participants.
